How do I know this will actually close?

It's a fair question, and the honest answer starts with something you don't often hear from someone trying to buy your house: nothing is guaranteed.

If you've been through a sale before, traditional or otherwise, you already know the feeling. You get an accepted offer, you start mentally moving on, and then something stalls it, financing falls through, an inspection turns into a renegotiation, a title issue nobody saw coming. So when someone tells you they'll buy your house, it's completely fair to ask: how do I actually know this closes?

Here's the honest answer, not a reassurance script.

Nothing is guaranteed, and I won't tell you otherwise

No one can promise you with absolute certainty that any real estate deal closes, mine included. Title issues surface. Liens turn out to be more complicated than expected. Occasionally something about a property comes to light during due diligence that changes the picture. Any buyer who tells you their deal is 100% guaranteed to close, no matter what, isn't being straight with you.

What I can tell you honestly is what's actually in my control, and why it's in my own direct interest to get your deal to the finish line.

Why closing matters to me as much as it matters to you

Here's something worth understanding about how this actually works: I get paid by the end buyer, not by you. That means if your deal doesn't close, I don't make money either. I'm not paid for putting a property under contract, I'm paid for successfully getting it closed. That single fact lines up my interest with yours far more than people usually assume. I'm not motivated to let a deal sit or quietly fall apart, I'm motivated to work every problem that comes up until it's resolved, because a deal that doesn't close costs me too, not just you.

That's why, once your property is under contract, I'm actively working it, following up on title, staying on top of documentation, checking in with everyone involved, because letting things stall doesn't serve me any better than it serves you.

What "staying informed" actually looks like

A lot of the anxiety in this process comes from silence, not from problems themselves. Most people can handle hearing "we're still waiting on the title company," what's hard is not hearing anything at all and wondering if the deal quietly died. So here's what you can expect from me directly:

Regular check-ins, even when there's nothing new

If I checked on your title status this morning and we're still waiting, I'll tell you that. "Still waiting on title, checked again today" is a normal, expected update, not a bad sign, and you'll hear it from me rather than have to wonder.

Honest pricing feedback, not silence

If the number we're working with isn't matching what buyers in the current market are willing to pay, I'll tell you that directly, along with what's actually realistic, instead of letting a deal quietly stall without explanation.

Real visibility into where things stand

Whether it's a document we're waiting on, a title company delay, or a buyer detail that needs resolving, you'll know what's actually holding things up, not just that something is.

Why exposure to the market matters

When I put a property out to the investor network I work with, it's genuinely seen. Every serious investor active in this market has eyes on new deals as they come up. That matters for you because it means your property isn't sitting in front of one single buyer who can quietly walk away and leave you starting over, it's in front of a real, active pool of buyers looking for exactly this kind of opportunity. That visibility is part of what makes closing realistic rather than a hope.

The honest bottom line

I can't promise you zero risk, nobody honestly can. What I can promise is that I'm financially motivated to solve problems instead of let them sit, and that you'll hear from me along the way, even when the update is simply that we're still waiting.
